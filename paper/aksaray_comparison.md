# STL-as-reward (Aksaray 2016) vs. STL-as-decoder-bias (this work)

This note formalises the relationship between Aksaray, Jones, Kong, Schwager, and Belta's *Q-Learning for Robust Satisfaction of Signal Temporal Logic Specifications* (CDC 2016, DOI 10.1109/CDC.2016.7799279, pages 6565-6570; arXiv:1609.07409) and the gradient-guided sampler in `src/stl_seed/inference/gradient_guided.py`. The discrete-vocabulary straight-through bridge is treated in `paper/stlcgpp_comparison.md` (Proposition 2) and is not re-derived here; this document focuses on the training-time vs. inference-time axis.

## 1. Aksaray et al.'s setup

Aksaray et al. consider an MDP $\\mathcal{M} = (S, A, P, s_0)$ with finite state space $S$ obtained as a partition of a continuous workspace, finite action space $A$, unknown transition kernel $P : S \\times A \\to \\Delta(S)$, and an STL formula $\\varphi$ with bounded time horizon $H\_\\varphi$. Their objective (Section III) is to maximise the *expected space-robustness* $$ \\mathbb{E}_\\pi\\bigl\[\\rho(\\tau_\\pi, \\varphi)\\bigr\], \\qquad \\tau\_\\pi \\in S^{H\_\\varphi}, $$ where $\\tau\_\\pi$ is the trajectory induced by policy $\\pi$ from $s_0$. Because $\\rho$ is a non-Markovian functional of the whole trajectory, vanilla Q-learning does not apply directly; their key construction (Section III-B) is a $\\tau$-augmented MDP whose state at time $t$ is the *flag-state pair* $(s_t, q_t)$ with $q_t$ an ancillary token that tracks the partial-trajectory information needed to evaluate $\\rho$ at horizon end. On the augmented MDP, a Q-learning update with episodic terminal reward $r\_\\text{term} = \\rho(\\tau, \\varphi)$ converges (under standard Robbins-Monro step sizes) to the optimal Q-function $Q^\\star$ for the surrogate objective. Their Theorem 1 quantifies the suboptimality gap between the surrogate and the original expected-robustness objective.

The salient features for our comparison: (i) the algorithm is **training-time**, requiring repeated rollouts and a Q-table update; (ii) $\\rho$ enters only as a **scalar terminal reward** at the end of each episode, not as a per-step gradient; (iii) the action space is **discrete and finite** by construction (tabular Q-learning); (iv) no differentiable simulator is assumed or needed.

## 2. Our setup

The pipeline of `src/stl_seed/inference/gradient_guided.py` is summarised in `paper/inference_method.md` and `paper/landscape_theorem.md`. The agent is a *fixed* pretrained LLM $g_M$ (Qwen3-0.6B / 1.7B / 4B; arXiv:2505.09388); no policy parameters are updated. At decoding step $t \\in {1, \\ldots, H}$ the LLM emits logits $z_t \\in \\mathbb{R}^K$ over a discrete action vocabulary $V \\in \\mathbb{R}^{K \\times m}$. We construct the LLM's expected action $\\bar{u}_t = \\sum_k p_{t,k} V_k$ with $p_t = \\mathrm{softmax}(z_t)$, the partial-then-extrapolated control $\\hat{u}_t = (u_{1:t-1}, \\bar{u}_t, u_\\text{def}^{\\otimes(H-t)})$, and the simulator-and-evaluator gradient $$ g_t = \\nabla\_{\\bar{u}\_t}, J(\\hat{u}_t), \\qquad J = \\rho \\circ \\mathrm{Sim}(x_0, \\cdot), $$ through the JIT-traced Diffrax solve. The bias and the categorical sample are $$ b_{t,k} = \\lambda \\langle V_k - \\bar{u}\_t, g_t \\rangle, \\qquad a_t \\sim \\mathrm{softmax}\\bigl((z_t + b_t)/T\\bigr). $$ At $\\lambda = 0$ the bias vanishes and standard decoding is recovered exactly.

The salient features: (i) the algorithm is **inference-time**, with no parameter updates; (ii) $\\rho$ enters via its **gradient** $\\nabla\_{\\bar{u}_t} \\rho$, computed by autodiff through a differentiable ODE simulator; (iii) the action space is **discrete** ($V \\subset \\mathbb{R}^m$, $|V| = K$) but bridged to the continuous gradient by the straight-through projection $b_{t,k}$; (iv) a differentiable simulator is required.

## 3. Mathematical relationship

**Proposition (one-step gradient as a Taylor approximation of an inference-time Q-increment).** Fix a control history $u\_{1:t-1}$, the LLM logits $z_t$ with induced expected action $\\bar{u}_t$, and the default-extrapolation tail $u_\\text{def}^{\\otimes(H-t)}$. Define the *inference-time Q-function* $$ Q_t(V_k) := J\\bigl(u\_{1:t-1},, V_k,, u\_\\text{def}^{\\otimes(H-t)}\\bigr), \\qquad k \\in {1, \\ldots, K}, $$ i.e. the terminal STL robustness obtained by playing $V_k$ at step $t$ and the default thereafter. Then under the regularity assumptions A1-A2 of `paper/landscape_theorem.md` (locally Lipschitz $f$ with $L\_\\text{sim} = L_u e^{L_x T\_\\text{end}}$, Donzé-Maler 1-Lipschitz $\\rho$): $$ Q_t(V_k) - Q_t(\\bar{u}\_t) ;=; \\langle V_k - \\bar{u}_t,, g_t \\rangle ;+; R_k, \\qquad |R_k| \\le \\tfrac{1}{2} |V_k - \\bar{u}_t|^2 \\cdot \\sup_\\xi |\\nabla^2_u J(\\xi)|. \\tag{$\\ast$}$$ In particular, the bias $b_{t,k} = \\lambda \\langle V_k - \\bar{u}\_t, g_t \\rangle$ is exactly $\\lambda$ times the first-order Taylor approximation of the increment $Q_t(V_k) - Q_t(\\bar{u}\_t)$.

*Proof sketch.* Apply Taylor's theorem to the scalar map $u \\mapsto J(u\_{1:t-1}, u, u\_\\text{def}^{\\otimes(H-t)})$ at the basepoint $u = \\bar{u}\_t$. The first-order term is $\\langle V_k - \\bar{u}_t, \\nabla_u J(u_{1:t-1}, \\bar{u}_t, u_\\text{def}^{\\otimes(H-t)})\\rangle = \\langle V_k - \\bar{u}\_t, g_t\\rangle$; the remainder is bounded by the standard quadratic form in the Hessian-norm supremum over the line segment $\[\\bar{u}\_t, V_k\]$, finite by A1-A2 (Grönwall on the variational equation gives a finite Lipschitz constant for $\\nabla J$ on the bounded action box, hence a finite Hessian-norm sup on the compact segment). $\\square$

The interpretation: in Aksaray's discounted-MDP formalism, the increment $Q_t(V_k) - V_t$ at fixed history is precisely the *advantage* of action $V_k$, and Q-learning's update step shifts the policy toward actions with positive advantage. Our $b\_{t,k}$ is the linearised advantage at the LLM's expected action, computed *without* a learned Q-function. instead it is read off the simulator gradient at inference time. The trade-off is explicit: Aksaray pays for a value-function approximator and learns it from rollouts; we pay for a differentiable simulator and read the same advantage off the gradient.

## 4. Three points of novelty

**4.1. No training step.** Aksaray's Q-learning loop iterates Robbins-Monro updates $Q(s, a) \\leftarrow Q(s, a) + \\alpha\[r + \\gamma \\max\_{a'} Q(s', a') - Q(s, a)\]$ and requires $O(|S \\times Q| \\cdot |A|)$ visits for tabular convergence (their Section III-C). Our pipeline performs zero parameter updates; the advantage signal is computed once per decoding step from the simulator Jacobian. The cost analysis in `paper/inference_method.md` (one forward and one reverse Diffrax solve per step) replaces the entire Q-learning training budget.

**4.2. Discrete-vocabulary bridge via a straight-through estimator.** Aksaray's discrete action space is intrinsic to the tabular MDP. Ours is induced by an LLM's categorical decoding distribution over a vocabulary $V \\subset U$, while the gradient $g_t$ lives in the continuous tangent space $T\_{\\bar{u}\_t} U \\subseteq \\mathbb{R}^m$. The expected-action embedding $\\bar{u}_t = \\sum_k p_{t,k} V_k$ plus the Bengio-Léonard-Courville straight-through projection (arXiv:1308.3432) is what makes the continuous gradient land on a categorical logit. The full derivation is in `paper/stlcgpp_comparison.md` Proposition 2; we do not re-derive it here.

**4.3. LLM-decoding regime.** Aksaray's policy is a tabular function $\\pi : S \\times Q \\to A$ trained from scratch. Ours is a fixed pretrained LLM whose linguistic prior over action tokens is preserved as $z_t$ and merely *biased* by $b\_{t,k}$. The composition of (i) an open-domain language-model prior with (ii) a formal-spec gradient signal is absent from Aksaray; conversely, Aksaray's tabular convergence guarantees do not survive the LLM-decoding setting because $z_t$ is not optimised end-to-end. The two algorithms inhabit different regimes of the bias-variance frontier: Aksaray gives an asymptotically optimal tabular policy at training-time cost; we give an inference-time logit perturbation that preserves the LLM prior and reaches $S\_+$ under the smooth-regime conditions of `paper/landscape_theorem.md` Theorem (I).

## 5. Unification statement

`stl-seed` is what you get when you take Aksaray's STL-as-reward signal, apply it via differentiable simulators (STLCG / STLCG++ infrastructure; Leung 2020 arXiv:2008.00097, Hashemi 2025 arXiv:2501.04194), and bias an LLM at inference time instead of training a policy.

Equivalently, in the limit of one-step lookahead with a uniform LLM prior, $b\_{t,k}$ is a first-order Taylor approximation (eq. $\\ast$) of the per-step Q-increment that Aksaray's Q-learning would converge to in the analogous discrete-action MDP, computed by autodiff through the simulator rather than estimated from rollouts.

## 6. Honest scope

We do not claim to be the first to use STL $\\rho$ in any decoding-time setting. LogicGuard (Sun, Chen, Cai, Ehinger, Topcu 2025, arXiv:2507.03293) uses LTL satisfaction as a hard token-level vetoer at LLM decoding time; it acts on the *sign* of the formal-logic signal rather than its magnitude or gradient, and our regime-I rate bound (`paper/landscape_theorem.md` eq. (1)) explicitly depends on the spec margin $\\eta\_\\star$ that LogicGuard cannot exploit. Earlier RL-from-STL work beyond Aksaray includes Li et al. 2017 on truncated linear-temporal-logic robustness, Balakrishnan-Deshmukh 2019 on structured rewards, and Hasanbeig et al. 2019 on logically-constrained Q-learning; all are training-time.

The novel composition is: (formal STL with continuous magnitude) $\\times$ (per-step gradient bias on a fixed LLM) $\\times$ (discrete-vocabulary straight-through bridge) $\\times$ (scientific-control bio-ODE / glucose-insulin domain). Each factor in isolation has prior art. Their combination, with the $\\lambda = 0$ collapse-to-vanilla-decoding test (`tests/test_inference.py::test_gradient_guided_zero_lambda_matches_standard`), the matched-compute baseline (`paper/inference_method.md` cost analysis), and the empirical landscape characterisation (`paper/landscape_theorem.md` Theorem (I) vs. (II)), is what is new here.
