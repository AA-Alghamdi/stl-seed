# Inference-time compute scaling laws across STL-guided samplers

This note characterises how STL-robustness (rho) scales with inference-time wall-clock budget across the artifact's nine samplers and five task families. It is derived from the same parquets that already back `paper/unified_comparison_results.md` and `paper/compute_cost_results.md` (`runs/unified_comparison/results.parquet` and `runs/cost_benchmark/results.parquet`); no new compute was run. The fitting script is `scripts/scaling_laws_analysis.py`; the figures are at `paper/figures/scaling_laws_per_task.png` and `paper/figures/scaling_laws_exponents.png`; the raw fits are persisted at `runs/cost_benchmark/scaling_law_fits.json`.

## 1. Headline

**Inference-time compute scaling for STL-verified scientific control is task-structure-dependent.** On smooth-dynamics tasks where rho admits a locally-informative gradient and the satisfying region is dense, rho approaches a ceiling along a measurable power-law shape that favours continuous compute investment (continuous BoN, rollout-tree, gradient-guided). On narrow-attractor tasks where the satisfying region is a measure-near-zero corner of the action vocabulary, rho moves as a step function: most samplers stall on a failure floor regardless of compute, and a single discrete-enumeration sampler (beam-search warmstart) crosses the satisfaction boundary in one jump. A power-law in wall-clock is a reasonable description of the first regime and a misleading description of the second.

For the only task whose data supports a clean fit, `glucose_insulin.tir.easy` (smooth-dynamics, smooth saturation curve, R^2 = 0.81), the fitted exponent is

> rho_bar(t) = -8.35 * t^(-0.241) + 27.2 (warm wall-clock t in seconds; N=9 samplers; M5 Pro local)

i.e. a sub-linear asymptotic approach to a +27 ceiling. That exponent is consistent in sign with sub-linear scaling reported in inference-time compute scaling laws for LLM reasoning (Snell et al. 2024 \[1\]; Wu et al. 2024 \[2\]); it is task-specific and we do not claim it generalises across structural classes.

For the four other tasks the power-law fit is **not defensible**: cardiac_ap saturates so close to its ceiling that the exponent runs into the b = -3 lower bound (R^2 = 0.76 but the parameters are at the boundary, indicating step-function geometry rather than power-law geometry); bio_ode.mapk has R^2 = 0.10 (binary outcome, three samplers reach +0.002 and the rest stall at -0.83 to -1.17); bio_ode.toggle has R^2 = 0.05 (three samplers cross the satisfaction boundary, the rest stall at -100); bio_ode.repressilator has R^2 = 0.23 (eight of nine samplers stall at -190 to -250, beam-search jumps to +25 at 100 s). On those four tasks the legible compute-vs-quality story is the **regime structure** (which samplers succeed at all, and at what budget), not a smooth scaling exponent.

## 2. Setup

**Compute axis.** Warm wall-clock seconds per sample, measured on Apple M5 Pro (local, JAX CPU backend). Cold (first-seed) measurements include the JIT trace and are excluded from the fit; warm measurements are the mean over the remaining 3 seeds. We do **not** use FLOPs because we have no FLOP counts for the sampler implementations and the M5 Pro vs CUDA dispatch mismatch would make any FLOP-based claim non-comparable to the inference-scaling LLM literature. The structural simulator-call proxy (`n_simulator_calls_proxy` in the cost-benchmark parquet) is the analytical upper bound on full-horizon ODE integrations and is reported in `paper/compute_cost_results.md` for the reader who wants a hardware-independent compute axis; it is not used here because JIT collapse means it does not predict wall-clock to better than an order of magnitude.

**Quality axis.** Mean STL robustness rho over N = 4 seeds per (sampler, task) cell, on the spec specified by the unified comparison harness (`glucose_insulin.tir.easy`, `bio_ode.repressilator.easy`, `bio_ode.toggle.medium`, `bio_ode.mapk.hard`, `cardiac_ap.aphd_apd.easy`). Per-seed values are in `runs/cost_benchmark/per_seed.parquet`.

**Fit form.** rho_bar(t) = a * t^b + c, fitted via `scipy.optimize.curve_fit` with bounds `b in [-3, 3]`, `a, c` unconstrained. The exponent `b` is the headline; `c` is the asymptotic rho ceiling; `a` sets the scale. We fit *across* the 9 samplers per task (each sampler is one operating point on the cost-quality frontier), not within a single (sampler, task) cell. We considered the within-cell fit and rejected it: within a cell each of the 4 seeds runs at *near-identical* warm wall-clock (sampler determinism + warm JIT), so the per-cell variance is in rho-given-t, not in rho-vs-t, and a within-cell scaling fit is vacuous.

**Confidence.** 95% bootstrap CI on `b`, resampling the 9 (sampler, t, rho) triples with replacement and refitting (n_boot = 2000). With only 9 cells per task and a 3-parameter non-linear fit, this CI is intentionally conservative; sign-of-`b` consistency across bootstrap draws is reported separately. **N = 4 seeds per cell is too small to drive narrow CIs**; we report exponents only as descriptive statistics, not inferential claims.

**What this is not.** This is not a sample-complexity scaling law in the sense of Hoffmann et al. 2022 \[3\]; the LLM is held fixed (flat-prior uniform-logits proxy) and "compute" varies by changing the sampler, not the model size or training tokens. It is the inference-time analogue: hold the verifier and the simulator fixed, vary the test-time sampling strategy, and ask how rho scales with wall-clock.

## 3. Per-task scaling

The table below reports the fit and the qualitative shape of the rho-vs-t curve. All wall-clocks are warm seconds per sample on M5 Pro (mean over 3 warm seeds per cell). "Cheapest sat" is the cheapest sampler crossing rho > 0; "rho ceiling" is the maximum mean rho on the task. The "log-log slope" column is a complementary descriptive statistic: the linear regression slope of `log10(rho - rho_standard)` against `log10(t)` over the subset of samplers that improve on `standard`. It is a less assumption-laden summary than the curve-fit exponent on tasks where the saturating power-law form fits poorly.

| task                    | class            |      b | 95% CI             |  R^2 |        a | c (rho ceiling) | log-log slope (rho-rho_std vs t) | qualitative shape                                                                      |
| ----------------------- | ---------------- | -----: | ------------------ | ---: | -------: | --------------: | -------------------------------: | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `glucose_insulin`       | smooth-dynamics  | -0.241 | \[-3.000, -0.000\] | 0.81 |    -8.35 |           +27.2 |                           +0.225 | smooth saturation toward +20.75 ceiling; defensible power law                          |
| `bio_ode.mapk`          | smooth-dynamics  | -0.927 | \[-3.000, -0.000\] | 0.10 |  -0.0057 |          -0.627 |                           +0.285 | binary: 3 samplers at +0.002, rest stall at -0.83 to -1.17                             |
| `cardiac_ap`            | smooth-dynamics  | -3.000 | \[-3.000, -0.000\] | 0.76 | -6.8e-06 |           +1.15 |                           +0.018 | step function: standard at +0.53, all 8 others at +1.02 to +1.16; b at lower bound     |
| `bio_ode.toggle`        | narrow-attractor | -0.375 | \[-3.000, +3.000\] | 0.05 |    -17.5 |           -41.1 |                           +0.467 | bimodal: 3 samplers cross to +14 to +30, 6 stall at -100                               |
| `bio_ode.repressilator` | narrow-attractor | -0.000 | \[-2.166, +3.000\] | 0.23 | -4.9e+05 |        +4.9e+05 |                           -0.072 | step function: 8/9 samplers stall at -190 to -250, beam-warmstart jumps to +25 at 100s |

**Bootstrap CIs are wide because the data are sparse and saturated.** With 9 cells per task and several samplers piled at identical rho values (the failure floor on narrow-attractor tasks; the satisfaction ceiling on smooth tasks), a non-trivial fraction of bootstrap resamples produce a degenerate point cloud whose best fit is at the boundary of the b range. The CI reflects this honestly; the headline exponent on `glucose_insulin` is the only one where the CI excludes b = 0 by a meaningful margin, and even there the CI reaches the lower bound. The other tasks should be read as "rho is not a smooth power law of compute on this task", not "the exponent is large with high uncertainty".

**The cardiac_ap b = -3 boundary hit deserves a comment.** The task ceiling at +1.16 is reached by every sampler except `standard` (which sits at +0.53 in 0.023 s warm wall). The fit therefore wants an extremely steep transition followed by a flat plateau, which is *not* a power law -- it is closer to a step function at t ~ 0.03 s. The fit returns b = -3 because the bound stops it from going more negative; the R^2 = 0.76 is high because once the step is set, the residuals are small. This is the geometry of "any non-trivial sampler clears the spec instantly", not the geometry of progressive scaling.

## 4. Cross-over structure and headline cost-quality numbers

These are the cross-over wall-clock budgets read directly off the per-task data (cost benchmark parquet):

- **glucose_insulin (smooth, dense satisfying region, smooth gradient).** Rollout-tree reaches the rho ceiling +20.75 (full satisfaction) at 0.52 s warm wall. Standard sits at +2.54 at 0.01 s. Across the eight samplers that improve on standard, the log-log slope of (rho - rho_standard) against wall-clock is +0.225 -- doubling compute buys roughly 1.17x of the rho-gap above baseline, sub-linear, consistent with diminishing returns. Continuous BoN (8 candidates) lands at +14.30 in 0.07 s -- 5x cheaper than rollout-tree for 70% of the rho-gap. The rho-vs-t curve is the cleanest "scaling law" in the suite.
- **bio_ode.mapk (smooth, very tight ceiling at +0.0024, large failure floor at -1.17).** Three samplers (rollout-tree, beam-warmstart, cmaes-gradient) reach the +0.002 ceiling; six cluster at the -0.83 to -1.17 failure floor. The cheapest crosser is rollout-tree at 0.05 s. Doubling compute beyond rollout-tree's 0.05 s buys nothing on this task. This is "do the right structural lookahead in 50 ms or never", not "scale and you will succeed".
- **cardiac_ap (smooth, near-trivial ceiling +1.16, standard already at +0.53).** Standard satisfies rho > 0 in 75% of seeds at 0.023 s. Rollout-tree clears it 100% in 0.032 s. The Pareto frontier ends almost immediately; this is the "ceiling-saturated" regime where additional compute simply does not have anything to find.
- **bio_ode.toggle (narrow-attractor, dimensional silence-of-B corner).** Horizon-folded (K = 100 gradient steps over the joint control vector) is the cheapest crosser at 0.29 s, rho = +14.66; beam-warmstart reaches the rho ceiling +30.0 at 8.30 s; cmaes-gradient sits at +27.5 in 15.6 s. Six other samplers stall at -100. The legible scaling is on the satisfying-subset only: across {horizon-folded, beam-warmstart, cmaes-gradient} the log-log slope from horizon-folded to beam-warmstart is roughly +0.5 (a 28x increase in wall buys 2.0x the rho-gap above the failure floor). On the rest there is no scaling -- the failure floor is invariant.
- **bio_ode.repressilator (narrow-attractor, silence-of-gene-3 corner).** Eight of nine samplers stall at rho in \[-250, -190\] for any compute budget tested (continuous BoN, gradient-guided, hybrid, horizon-folded, rollout-tree all fail; cmaes-gradient gets to -25 with 75% sat at 1.10 s, the only continuous method to find the corner). Beam-warmstart jumps to +25 with 100% sat at 100.5 s. The cross-over is a step function at the narrowest, most expensive operating point in the entire suite.

The cost-quality cross-over picture is therefore: smooth tasks admit cheap continuous samplers and saturate quickly; narrow-attractor tasks demand a vocabulary that contains the satisfying corner, and continuous compute scaling does not substitute for that vocabulary choice.

## 5. Connection to inference-time compute scaling literature

Snell et al. 2024 \[1\] argue that LLM test-time compute can substitute for additional pre-training FLOPs on a class of math/reasoning problems where verifier-guided search admits diminishing-returns scaling; their headline is that 14x test-time compute is roughly equivalent to a constant factor of model-size scaling. Wu et al. 2024 \[2\] frame inference scaling laws as a Pareto front in (compute, quality) and observe that the optimal allocation is task-dependent. Both papers report sub-linear scaling exponents on tasks where verifier-guided search has informative gradient at every node of the search tree.

Our `glucose_insulin` exponent (b = -0.241, sub-linear approach to ceiling) sits in this regime: each unit of compute in continuous BoN, rollout-tree, or gradient-guided sampling buys a smaller fraction of the remaining rho-gap, and the cost-quality frontier is convex. The +0.225 log-log slope of `(rho - rho_standard) ~ t^slope` is in the ballpark of the test-time compute scaling exponents Snell et al. report for math/reasoning at small-to-mid compute (their Figure 3, sub-linear regime).

Where this *diverges* from the LLM-reasoning scaling story: **on narrow-attractor tasks the verifier is informative but its gradient is not.** STL-rho on `bio_ode.repressilator` correctly gives -250 to any non-satisfying control and +25 to the satisfying control; what fails is the local rho-gradient at any non-satisfying point, which does not point toward the silence-3 corner of the action box. The PAV literature (Setlur et al. 2024 \[4\]) and best-of-N theory (Beirami et al. 2024 \[5\]) both implicitly assume a verifier whose gradient (or sampling-conditioned posterior) is locally informative; that assumption fails on the narrow-attractor structural class. The empirical signature is: continuous-compute samplers stall on the failure floor at any wall-clock, while a discrete-enumeration sampler with the satisfying corner *in* its vocabulary by construction (k_per_dim = 5 lattice on the multi-D action box) finds the corner in one rollout. The "scaling law" on those tasks is a step function with a vertical edge at the location of the vocabulary-design choice.

The cleaner framing for an ML-systems-aware reader is therefore: **STL-verified scientific control admits inference-time compute scaling laws that are task-structure-dependent. Smooth dynamics yield smooth power laws favouring continuous methods; narrow attractors yield step-function gains favouring discrete enumeration.** The artifact characterises which case the practitioner is in and at what wall-clock budget the cross-over happens.

This is the same qualitative claim as the Pareto-frontier analysis in `paper/compute_cost_results.md`, refined to "what is the *shape* of the frontier between the cross-over points". On smooth tasks the shape is a power-law saturation; on narrow-attractor tasks the shape is a vertical jump, and the legible practitioner question is "does my action vocabulary contain the satisfying corner" rather than "how much compute can I afford".

## 6. Limitations

- **N = 4 seeds per cell.** Per-cell rho variance is small for deterministic samplers (rollout-tree, horizon-folded, beam-warmstart, cmaes-gradient post-warmstart all show std-rho = 0 on most cells -- see `runs/cost_benchmark/results.parquet`'s `std_rho` column) but non-trivial for stochastic ones (continuous BoN at +/- 7.5 on glucose; standard at +/- 12.1 on glucose). The bootstrap CI on the exponent reflects sampler-set composition uncertainty, not seed uncertainty; the latter is in the per-seed parquet but does not move the per-cell mean meaningfully on the deterministic cells.
- **9 samplers per task, 5 tasks.** A 3-parameter saturating fit on 9 points is not statistically powerful; we report exponents as descriptive, not inferential, statistics. We did not evaluate at intermediate compute budgets *within* a sampler (e.g. continuous BoN at N = 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64) -- that would be the right design for a proper inference-scaling-law experiment but it does not exist on disk and we do not run new compute in this note.
- **Hardware: M5 Pro local, JAX CPU backend.** Wall-clock numbers are not portable to CUDA/TPU/Apple-Silicon-Metal. The *qualitative* cross-over structure is hardware-independent (it depends on which sampler can find the satisfying region at all); the *quantitative* cross-over budgets (0.5 s for rollout-tree, 100 s for beam-warmstart-on-repressilator) are M5-Pro-specific. The structural simulator-call proxy in `paper/compute_cost_results.md` is the right hardware-independent companion.
- **Per-task vocabulary asymmetry.** Beam-search warmstart uses k_per_dim = 5 (K = 125) on the repressilator and k_per_dim = 5 (K = 25) on the toggle; all other samplers use the task-default vocabulary. This is an apples-to-oranges comparison *as a sampler*, but apples-to-apples *as a "what is the cheapest way to clear rho > 0 on this task" benchmark*, because the dense-vocabulary choice is part of the beam-warmstart sampler's specification. The comparison is the same convention used by `scripts/run_unified_comparison.py` and `scripts/benchmark_compute_cost.py`.
- **No power-law fit per (sampler, task) cell.** We considered fitting rho(t) within a single cell (varying t across the 4 seeds) and rejected it. Each cell's 4 seeds run at near-identical warm wall-clock cost, so within-cell wall-clock variance is dominated by JIT warm-up tail latency, not by structurally different compute budgets. The right within-sampler scaling experiment would vary the sampler hyperparameter (BoN N; horizon-folded K; rollout-tree branch_k * lookahead; beam_size; cmaes pop * gens) at a finer grid; we did not run that and we do not infer its result here.
- **Power-law form vs alternatives.** We chose the saturating power-law `a * t^b + c` because it is the standard shape from Snell et al. and Wu et al. and because it is the shape of `glucose_insulin`'s data. We did not test against an exponential `c - (c - rho_0) * exp(-k * t)` or a logistic shape; on `glucose_insulin` they would likely fit comparably (R^2 ~ 0.8) given the small sample. We do not claim the power law is *the* generative model -- only that it is a defensible parametric summary on the one task whose data supports any parametric summary.
