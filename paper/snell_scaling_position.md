# Position note: stl-seed scaling laws against Snell et al. 2024

This note positions the stl-seed inference-time compute scaling result against Snell et al. 2024 \[1\] (arXiv:2408.03314), the reference paper for inference-time compute scaling on LLM math reasoning. The intended framing for the paper draft is: STL-verified scientific control admits the same qualitative scaling regime as verifier-guided LLM math reasoning, sub-linear diminishing returns under verifier-guided search, with a comparable compute-quality cross-over budget. The exponent comparison is suggestive at the data we have, not statistically rigorous; this note states that limit explicitly.

The companion figure is at `paper/figures/snell_position.png` (generated by `scripts/_make_snell_position.py`, 200 DPI). The headline scaling fit is from `paper/scaling_laws.md` and `runs/cost_benchmark/scaling_law_fits.json`.

## 1. Snell et al. 2024 recap

Snell, Lee, Xu, Kumar \[1\] study how to allocate test-time compute on a fixed base LLM (PaLM 2-S\*) on the MATH benchmark, with a process reward model (PRM) trained via Monte-Carlo rollouts in the style of Wang et al. The two test-time strategies are (a) best-of-N with PRM-weighted selection (and PRM-guided beam search and lookahead-search variants), and (b) sequential revisions, where a finetuned model edits its own answer. The headline claim, repeated in the abstract and Section 1, is that with optimal test-time-compute allocation conditioned on prompt difficulty, a small base model with extra inference compute can match or beat a 14x larger pretrained model on MATH problems where the small model already has non-trivial success rates. A second headline number, from Section 5.3 (search) and Section 6.2 (revisions), is that compute-optimal allocation is "up to 4x more efficient" than a uniform best-of-N baseline at iso-accuracy: 16 generations under compute-optimal search are roughly equivalent to 64 best-of-N generations; 64 revisions are roughly equivalent to 256 best-of-N samples.

Two facts about the paper are load-bearing for this position note. First, Snell et al. do not report explicit power-law exponents of the form `accuracy ~ N^b`; their plots (Figures 4, 8, 9) use linear-x axes and quote compute-equivalence ratios (4x, 14x), not slopes. Any "Snell exponent" in the literature is downstream-derived by reading off iso-accuracy contours from their figures. Second, their compute axis is generation count (or FLOPs computed via a 6ND/2ND approximation for pretrain/inference), not wall-clock; their quality axis is MATH accuracy, a binary-outcome benchmark with discrete pass/fail per problem. Both of these differ from our axes (warm wall-clock seconds; continuous STL robustness rho).

## 2. Our scaling result restated

From `paper/scaling_laws.md`, on `glucose_insulin.tir.easy`, fitting `rho_bar(t) = a t^b + c` across the 9 samplers (each sampler is one operating point on the cost-quality frontier; t is warm wall-clock seconds per sample on Apple M5 Pro, JAX CPU backend) gives

> rho_bar(t) = -8.35 * t^(-0.241) + 27.23, R^2 = 0.814, N = 9 cells.

The fit is sub-linear-saturating: rho approaches a +27 asymptote with diminishing returns in compute, and the rho ceiling on this task (the value reached by the strongest samplers) is +20.75. The bootstrap CI on `b` is \[-3.000, -0.0001\] from 2000 resamples (95% CI; lower bound at the fit constraint, upper bound near zero). The CI excludes `b = 0` in 100% of bootstrap draws, but is wide because of small N.

A complementary, less-parametric, summary is the log-log slope of "fraction of rho-gap closed above the standard sampler" vs warm wall-clock across the 8 non-standard samplers: slope = +0.225. This is the metric that lines up with Snell's "fraction of accuracy headroom captured by extra test-time compute" frame; doubling wall-clock buys roughly a factor of 2^0.225 ~ 1.17x of the rho-gap above baseline.

Both numbers say the same thing: on a smooth-dynamics task with a dense satisfying region and a locally informative rho-gradient, our cost-quality frontier is convex with sub-linear returns, and continuous samplers (continuous BoN, gradient-guided, rollout-tree) sit on the frontier.

## 3. Quantitative position

The figure `paper/figures/snell_position.png` has two panels. Left: the raw `(t, rho)` data with the saturating power-law fit; rho saturates at +20.75 between rollout-tree (0.52 s) and beam-warmstart (1.46 s). Right: the same data on log-log axes as "fraction of rho-gap closed above standard", with our empirical slope of +0.225 plotted alongside two stylized reference lines at slope +0.30 and +0.40, the band a careful reader would derive from Snell et al.'s Figures 4 and 8 by reading off iso-accuracy contours. The reference lines are labeled "implied" in the figure and are not numbers Snell et al. quote.

The quantitative comparison is therefore:

| quantity                                       | stl-seed (this work)                                                          | Snell et al. 2024 \[1\]               |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| benchmark                                      | glucose_insulin.tir.easy                                                      | MATH (Hendrycks et al.)               |
| compute axis                                   | warm wall-clock (s)                                                           | generation count / FLOPs              |
| quality axis                                   | continuous STL rho                                                            | binary MATH accuracy                  |
| verifier                                       | formal STL + ODE simulator                                                    | trained PRM (MC-rollout supervision)  |
| saturating power-law fit b (rho ~ t^b + c)     | -0.241 (R^2 = 0.81)                                                           | not reported                          |
| log-log slope on fraction-of-headroom-captured | +0.225                                                                        | not reported (implied ~ +0.3 to +0.4) |
| best-of-N to compute-optimal cross-over ratio  | ~ 8x (continuous BoN at 0.07 s vs rollout-tree at 0.52 s for 70% of headroom) | 4x (Sections 5.3, 6.2)                |

The cross-over-ratio comparison is the cleanest apples-to-apples number because both works report it. Snell quotes 4x compute reduction at iso-accuracy from compute-optimal allocation (16 vs 64 search; 64 vs 256 revisions). Continuous BoN (N=8) on glucose_insulin lands at +14.30 rho in 0.07 s; rollout-tree reaches the +20.75 ceiling in 0.52 s. The "first-knee" cross-over from continuous BoN to rollout-tree is roughly 7-8x in wall-clock for the last 30% of the rho-gap, which is the same order of magnitude as Snell's 4x.

The exponent comparison is suggestive but should be read with care. Our +0.225 log-log slope is slightly shallower than the +0.30 to +0.40 band most readers infer from Snell's figures, which is consistent with the idea that STL-verified scientific control has a tighter ceiling (full satisfaction at rho ~ +20.75 is a measure-positive set, not the open-ended pass-rate axis Snell uses); once the satisfying set is reached, further compute cannot improve rho. Both works are in the regime "verifier-guided search admits diminishing-returns power-law-shape scaling on smooth quality-vs-compute curves".

## 4. Caveats and statistical scope

This comparison is descriptive, not inferential. The data does not support a strong claim that the two exponents are statistically equivalent.

- N is too small. We have 9 samplers x 4 seeds x 1 task in the smooth-dynamics regime that supports a clean fit. Snell et al. report on hundreds of MATH problems across multiple difficulty levels and multiple base-model scales. Our bootstrap CI on `b` is wide (`[-3.00, -0.0001]`) and reaches the fit-constraint lower bound; the sign of `b` is robust (negative in 100% of resamples) but the magnitude is not.
- Compute axes are not directly comparable. Snell uses generation count and a FLOPs-equivalence approximation; we use M5 Pro warm wall-clock seconds. Wall-clock includes JIT and dispatch overhead that are not present in a generation-count axis. The structural-simulator-call proxy in `paper/compute_cost_results.md` is closer to a hardware-independent compute axis but is not used here.
- Snell does not publish the reference exponent. The +0.30 to +0.40 band on the figure's right panel is the band a careful reader infers from Snell's Figures 4 and 8 by reading iso-accuracy contours; it is not a number quoted in their text. Using their compute-equivalence ratios (4x and 14x) as the comparison anchor is more defensible, and is the comparison we lead with in the table.
- The four other tasks in our suite do not admit a clean power-law fit. `bio_ode.mapk` (R^2 = 0.10), `bio_ode.toggle` (R^2 = 0.05), `bio_ode.repressilator` (R^2 = 0.23), and `cardiac_ap` (R^2 = 0.76 but b at the lower bound, indicating a step-function rather than power-law shape). The "same regime as Snell" claim applies only to the smooth-dynamics, dense-satisfying-set, locally-informative-rho-gradient subset of our task suite.
- Verifier informativeness is the load-bearing assumption. Snell's PRM has informative process supervision at every step of an LLM rollout. Our STL rho has informative gradient on smooth tasks but not on narrow-attractor tasks (see `paper/scaling_laws.md` Section 5). Where rho-gradient is locally uninformative, no compute-scaling regime emerges, and a discrete-enumeration sampler with the satisfying corner in its vocabulary by construction is the right structural answer.

## 5. Implications for STL-verified inference-time scaling

The defensible takeaway is structural, not numerical. The Snell et al. story, "verifier-guided search lets test-time compute substitute for pretraining FLOPs along a sub-linear diminishing-returns curve", carries over to STL-verified scientific control on the smooth-dynamics regime, with the same qualitative shape (saturating sub-linear in compute), the same order of magnitude on the compute-equivalence cross-over (4x for them, ~ 8x for us at the analogous knee), and the same boundary condition (the regime breaks where the verifier's local gradient becomes uninformative). What this buys for an ML-systems paper:

First, it is the right citation hook into the inference-scaling literature. STL-verified scientific control is a natural extension of the verifier-guided search story to a domain where the verifier is formal (an STL temporal-logic formula evaluated against an ODE trajectory) rather than learned (a PRM trained on rollout-correctness estimates). The structural fact, "the form of the scaling curve depends on the verifier's local informativeness, not on whether it is learned or formal", is the load-bearing claim our scaling-laws note can support and is the claim that will land with reviewers familiar with Snell et al.

Second, it sharpens the ablation question. Snell's 4x compute-reduction comes from compute-optimal allocation (i.e., choosing search vs revisions vs best-of-N as a function of problem difficulty). Our 8x cross-over from continuous BoN to rollout-tree is the same flavor: choosing the right sampler at the right operating point on the cost-quality frontier. The natural follow-up is the within-sampler scaling experiment we flagged as future work in `paper/scaling_laws.md` (continuous BoN at N = 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64; horizon-folded at K = 25, 50, 100, 200, 400; etc.); that experiment, if run, would let us report a within-sampler exponent directly comparable to a within-method Snell exponent and would close the gap between "qualitative regime match" and "quantitative exponent match".

Third, it identifies the structural regime where the analogy breaks. On narrow-attractor tasks (`bio_ode.repressilator`, `bio_ode.toggle`), no continuous-compute scaling regime exists; the scaling "curve" is a step function at the wall-clock budget where the action vocabulary first contains the satisfying corner. This is the right counterexample to put on the same axis as the smooth-task scaling claim, because it isolates the load-bearing condition of the Snell-regime analogy: verifier-gradient local informativeness, not verifier identity.

The bottom line for the paper draft: the right one-sentence positioning is "stl-seed extends the inference-time compute-scaling regime of Snell et al. 2024 from PRM-verified LLM math reasoning to STL-verified scientific control on smooth-dynamics tasks, with a comparable cross-over compute ratio and a comparable diminishing-returns shape; the regime breaks predictably where STL rho's local gradient is uninformative, which is a structural property of the task, not of the verifier or the sampler". That sentence is supported by the data we have.
